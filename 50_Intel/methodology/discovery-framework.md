# The Convergence Framework for Enterprise Cyber Discovery

## 1. The Name

**Convergence Discovery** — a framework that recognizes cyber deals converge at the point where business risk becomes board-visible and the buyer has moved from "what could go wrong" to "what are we buying to stay standing if it does."

## 2. The Thesis

Cyber discovery differs fundamentally from generic SaaS discovery in one way: the prospect is not shopping for a capability, they are building a defense against a named failure scenario they can articulate. The most common silence in cyber (113 of Onyxia's 114 losses) happens not because competitors win, but because the buyer never names the failure they fear. Your job in discovery is to move the conversation from regulatory compliance and process artifacts to the specific, emotionally grounded scenario that unlocks budget and urgency.

## 3. The Phases

### Phase 1: The Failure Scenario Trigger
**Entry criteria:** Initial conversation with CISO or GRC Director. Deal size unconfirmed.
**Output to exit:** CISO articulates a named failure scenario—data exfil, ransomware impact, third-party breach—in their own words, not your terminology.
**Disqualifiers:** CISO cannot describe what "bad" looks like at their org. Deflection to compliance language ("we need SOC 2 because our largest customer requires it"). No access to economic buyer.
**Voice example:** "Walk me through what keeps you up. Not the regulatory thing—the scenario that if it happened on your watch, how would you explain it to the board?"

### Phase 2: The Gap Confession
**Entry criteria:** Failure scenario named. CISO is emotionally present.
**Output to exit:** CISO describes what they have today, what it does well, where it visibly fails to stop the scenario they just named.
**Disqualifiers:** CISO defaults to vendor/tool language ("our SIEM isn't seeing..."). No admission of a specific gap. Attempt to sell feature fit before gap is locked.
**Voice example:** "You said exfil. Today, if lateral movement starts in your network, what do you see first? Walk me through the moment you realize it's happening."

### Phase 3: The Impact Unlock
**Entry criteria:** Gap clearly articulated.
**Output to exit:** CISO quantifies what the failure scenario costs—financial, operational, personal credibility (with board, with CEO, with peers).
**Disqualifiers:** CISO remains in abstract terms ("it would be bad"). No number, no timeline, no stakeholder consequence. Rephrasing your impact questions instead of naming their own.
**Voice example:** "If you missed a breach like that—detection delay, containment delay—who tells the board? What does that conversation look like for you?"

### Phase 4: The Buying Authority Map
**Entry criteria:** Failure scenario + gap + impact are locked.
**Output to exit:** CISO names who writes the check, who blocks the check, and who the board talks to first (often CFO or board risk committee, not CISO).
**Disqualifiers:** CISO says "I'll just buy it" or "finance always approves." No procurement process articulation. No mention of board or CFO involvement. Assumption that CISO has autonomous budget.
**Voice example:** "Money for this kind of fix—where does it come from? Is it part of your annual budget, or is this different? Who else has to sign off before you can commit?"

### Phase 5: The Champion Lockdown
**Entry criteria:** Authority map is clear. You have identified the CISO as the champion.
**Output to exit:** CISO commits to a specific action—a follow-up call, a POC design, a vendor meeting with CFO present—with a date and attendees named.
**Disqualifiers:** CISO says "let me think about it" or "I'll need to review internally." No next step agreed. No second stakeholder invited to conversation.
**Voice example:** "Here's what I'm hearing we should do: you and I agree on what a pilot looks like, and then you bring in [CFO/board risk chair] so they hear directly from me what we're testing and why. Can we do that call next Tuesday?"

### Phase 6: The CFO/Board Warm-Up (if deal is $200K+ ACV)
**Entry criteria:** CISO is armed and ready to be a champion. Procurement/CFO process is clear.
**Output to exit:** One indirect conversation or email touch with CFO or board risk sponsor to pre-surface the failure scenario and the dollar ask, so CISO can advocate without surprising finance.
**Disqualifiers:** Attempting to pitch to CFO directly without CISO present. Leading with ROI language instead of risk mitigation. Skipping this step and letting CISO hit finance alone (they will lose the deal to silence).
**Voice example:** (To CISO, separately) "When you loop in your CFO, they're likely to ask: what happens if we do nothing? Help me draft a note that lands that question first, so your CFO's team isn't asking you why you want to spend this."

## 4. The Qualifying Checklist

Before advancing from Phase 3 to Phase 4, answer yes/no:

- [ ] Has the CISO described the failure scenario in their own words, without prompting?
- [ ] Can the CISO articulate the gap between what they have today and what would stop that failure?
- [ ] Has the CISO named a financial or operational impact (not compliance risk)?
- [ ] Does the CISO see Onyxia's approach as materially different from what they're running now?
- [ ] Have you identified the economic buyer (CFO, board risk chair, COO) who controls the budget?
- [ ] Is the CISO willing to loop in at least one other stakeholder on a follow-up call?
- [ ] Does the CISO have procurement authority, or is there an explicit approval process you can map?

**Deal entry rule:** Do not move to Phase 4 unless at least 6 of 7 items are checked. If not, stay in Phase 3 (the gap phase) or disqualify.

## 5. The Language Anchors

### Opener Questions (Early Discovery)
- "What does a bad outcome look like on your watch?"
- "Walk me through your last security incident—or near-miss. What did you learn?"
- "If I asked your board what keeps the CISO up at night, what would they say?"

### Transition Questions (Gap to Impact)
- "Today, if that scenario started happening, what would you see first?"
- "How long would it take you to know there was a problem?"
- "Once you knew, what happens next—who do you call, and how long until the board finds out?"

### Testing-for-Pain Questions (Impact Unlock)
- "If you missed that detection and it hit the news, how do you explain it to your board?"
- "What would that do to your credibility with your CEO?"
- "How much would that cost—in incident response, regulatory fines, customer notifications, whatever's relevant to you?"

### Authority-Mapping Questions (Phase 4)
- "When you've approved a security investment before, how did the money flow?"
- "Who else sits in on decisions like this?"
- "Has your CFO or board risk committee weighed in on tool purchases, or is that your call?"

### Champion Lock Questions (Phase 5)
- "If we can design a test that proves this works in your environment, are you the one who'd drive that forward?"
- "Who else needs to see this work before you commit?"
- "Can we set up a brief call where I explain the approach to your [CFO/risk sponsor], so you're not carrying the load alone?"

## 6. The Anti-Patterns

Never do these in discovery with a CISO:

1. **Never let the CISO define the problem in their tool's vocabulary.** If they say "our SIEM has visibility gaps," dig under that. What is the failure scenario the SIEM gap creates? Do not accept tool-speak as pain.

2. **Never assume the CISO has budget authority.** In mid-market and enterprise, 60%+ of cyber deals require CFO or board committee approval. Ask early, confirm late.

3. **Never sell the feature before the gap is locked.** Jumping to "Onyxia does X better" before the CISO admits their tool doesn't do X is a sale you've already lost to silence.

4. **Never ask "what are your priorities?" and accept a compliance answer.** Compliance is not a failure scenario. Dig into what happens if they fail compliance (regulatory fine, customer breach, board consequences). That's the real pain.

5. **Never let the CISO leave a call without committing to a next step.** A call that ends in "let me think about it" is not a qualified opportunity. You've moved them to interest but not commitment. Disqualify or stay in Phase 3.

6. **Never bring multiple stakeholders into a call before the CISO is ready.** If the CISO is not yet the champion, adding the CFO to an early call will bury the deal. Warm them up in isolation first.

7. **Never assume "no decision" means the deal is dead.** Most Onyxia losses are no-decision. The deal is asleep, not lost. Re-engage with a new scenario, new data, or a new buyer contact to reignite.

## 7. Measurement Hooks

You know Convergence Discovery is working when:

- **Call 2 CISO conversion rate:** 60%+ of discovery calls in Phase 1 advance to Phase 2 (CISO names the gap). Below 40%, your opener is not landing the failure scenario urgently enough.

- **MEDDPICC slot fill rate:** By end of Phase 5, you should have M (metrics: quantified impact), E (economic buyer: CFO/board identified), D (decision criteria: explicitly named failure scenario), D (decision process: procurement path mapped), I (identify pain: locked in Phase 3), C (champion: CISO committed), C (competition: not relevant; silence is the competitor). If you're missing any, the deal will stall.

- **Champion strength score (0-10):** After Phase 5, ask yourself: would this CISO defend this deal against internal skepticism? Does the CISO believe Onyxia is the only vendor who understands their specific failure scenario? Strength below 7 means the CISO is a supporter, not a champion.

- **Time to POC commitment:** From first discovery call to CFO/champion agreement on POC terms should be 3-4 weeks. If you're in discovery for 8+ weeks without a POC date locked, you're in a no-decision funnel. Disqualify or reset the scenario.
