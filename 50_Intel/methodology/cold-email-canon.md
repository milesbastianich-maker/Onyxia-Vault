# Cold-Email Canon

These are emails that work. Each one anchors on ONE spine pain from `pain-library.md`. Each line is annotated with what it's doing and why.

When the drafter writes a new cold email, the workflow is:

1. Pick the spine pain.
2. Find the canon example tagged to that spine pain.
3. Match the structural pattern, not the words.
4. Write the new email.
5. Read the new draft side-by-side with the canon example. Ask: does this read like the same hand wrote both? If no, rewrite.

Voice transfers by reading good writing, not by following rules. These seven examples are the dictionary.

---

## How to read each canon entry

Every entry has:
- **Spine pain:** which pain from `pain-library.md` this email leans on
- **Best for:** the contact / industry / tenure where this pattern lands
- **Email body (verbatim):** the actual text
- **Line-by-line annotation:** what each line is doing and why
- **15-year-old comprehension check:** what a 15yo gets after reading
- **Pattern abstract:** the structural skeleton you can transfer

## Canon 1 — Andrew Puller / MedStar / Subject: `160 hours a month`

**Spine pain:** #1 (manual reporting eating analyst hours)
**Best for:** Healthcare CISO at a multi-region hospital system. Mid-to-mature tenure. Not new-leader.
**Status:** Validated by Miles. Gmail sent. Canonical.

**Body:**

> Andrew,
>
> Reporting-layer burden is the quiet killer for CISOs at your scale. One of our healthcare customers (13,000+ employees, multi-region) put it this way:
>
> "With Onyxia, we are able to save at least 160 working hours a month that one full-time analyst would traditionally spend on manual reporting and instead, focus more time on our actual risk management strategy."
>
> Sivan Tehila, our CEO, was a CISO before building this.
>
> Worth 15 minutes next Tuesday or Thursday to compare notes?
>
> Miles Bastianich
> Onyxia Cyber

**Line-by-line:**

- **`Andrew,`** — first-name-only opener. No "Hi," no "Hope you're well." Drops one line of filler. Signals peer, not vendor.
- **`Reporting-layer burden is the quiet killer for CISOs at your scale.`** — the Pattern Statement. Names the pain in language the recipient already uses to himself. "Quiet killer" is concrete and a little blunt. "At your scale" makes it about HIM, not generic.
- **`One of our healthcare customers (13,000+ employees, multi-region) put it this way:`** — sets up the verbatim quote. "Healthcare customer" + "13,000+ employees" + "multi-region" = same shape as MedStar. Recipient sees themselves in the customer.
- **`"With Onyxia, we are able to save at least 160 working hours a month..."`** — verbatim customer quote. Block-formatted. The number does the work. 160 hours = 1 FTE. Recipient does the math instantly.
- **`Sivan Tehila, our CEO, was a CISO before building this.`** — the Sivan line. ONE sentence. Establishes peer credibility. Does not pitch Sivan or Onyxia. Just states a fact.
- **`Worth 15 minutes next Tuesday or Thursday to compare notes?`** — the ask. "Compare notes" = peer framing. "Tuesday or Thursday" = specific, scoped. Single binary question.
- **`Miles Bastianich / Onyxia Cyber`** — sign-off. (NOTE: per 2026-05-04 update, the canonical sign-off block is now `Cheers, / Miles Bastianich / Onyxia Cyber / 203-524-8880`. This canon example predates that lock. New drafts use the four-line block.)

**15-year-old gets:**
- Andrew's team spends a lot of time making reports by hand.
- Another hospital like Andrew's saved 160 hours a month using Onyxia.
- The CEO of Onyxia used to do Andrew's job.
- Miles wants 15 minutes to talk peer-to-peer.

**Pattern abstract:**
1. First-name comma open.
2. Pattern statement naming the pain in the recipient's language, with a "your scale" or "your shop" anchor.
3. Customer-quote setup: profile-match in parentheses.
4. Verbatim block-quote.
5. Sivan-as-former-CISO sentence.
6. Peer-framed ask, scoped to specific days.
7. Sign-off block.

>
> A healthcare CISO at a 13,000-employee system told us:
>

**Line-by-line:**

- **`Marlon,`** — first-name comma.
- **`Saw the announcement on Chris Tyrell joining as CIO yesterday.`** — concrete trigger. Names the new exec. Names the timing ("yesterday"). Recipient knows you read the announcement.
- **`New-CIO windows reset the board-reporting expectations in a hurry.`** — the Pattern Statement. "New-CIO windows" is industry shorthand. "Reset...in a hurry" frames the urgency. A 15yo gets: new boss, new questions, fast.
- **`A healthcare CISO at a 13,000-employee system told us:`** — same setup as Canon 1. Profile-match in the framing.
- **`"With Onyxia..."`** — verbatim block quote. Same one as Canon 1. Healthcare gets the 160-hour quote every time.
- **`Our CEO Sivan Tehila is a former CISO. She does a 20-minute peer call with folks navigating moments like this. Not a demo.`** — Sivan + peer call + explicit "Not a demo." The "not a demo" is the ask-modifier that lowers the cost of saying yes.

- Sign-off.

- Another hospital saved 160 hours a month with Onyxia.

**Pattern abstract:**
1. First-name comma.
2. Concrete trigger sentence with named person + date.
3. Pattern Statement naming the pressure the trigger creates.
4. Customer-quote setup with profile match.
5. Verbatim block-quote.
6. Sivan + peer-call frame + "not a demo" modifier.
7. Single-sentence ask.

> Onyxia Cyber

**Line-by-line:**

- **`Congrats on the CISO role at First Horizon.`** — acknowledges the trigger. Specific to her.
- **`Moving from GRC at Fidelity to owning the full security program at an $84B-asset bank is a different animal — especially with SEC cyber disclosure rules tightening the reporting window.`** — the Pattern Statement, but personalized. Names her PRIOR role (GRC at Fidelity) and her CURRENT role ($84B-asset bank). The cameo regulation (SEC cyber disclosure) is a SUPPORTING fact, not the spine. The spine is "this is a different animal."

## Canon 5 — Harshal / CWT (Yosef Adatto send) / Subject: `SEC Inquiry`

**Spine pain:** #3 (board-ready / disclosure clock)
**Best for:** Public-company CISO or VP InfoSec, regulation-timed (rule just enacted, deadline approaching). The canonical "value-first, no ask" play.
**Status:** Verified closed-won at $100K (CWT). Canonical.

**Body:**

> Hi Harshal,
>
> I hope you are using this time for some well deserved R&R.
>
> I wanted to share a resource with you for the upcoming SEC regulations enacted on December 18th. It contains a guide for how to ensure you maintain compliance and a checklist for what to do if your organization encounters a potential incident, and the actions you can take.
>
> Originally the team and I created this resource for our public enterprise customers to be a companion piece for our platform that continuously monitors, measures, and communicates the value of their security program to the business through AI and automation.
>
> I wanted to share it with you with zero upfront ask to see if it can provide value.
>
> You can read the checklist here: [SEC Checklist PDF link]
>
> Don't like clicking links? I'd be happy to show it to you when timely and relevant.
>
> Looking forward,
> Yosef

**Line-by-line:**

- **`Hi Harshal,`** — Yosef opens with "Hi"; Miles uses first-name-comma. Both work; Miles's is preferred for our voice.
- **`I hope you are using this time for some well deserved R&R.`** — seasonal nicety (sent before holidays). Miles can drop this; it's Yosef's voice, not Miles's.
- **`I wanted to share a resource with you for the upcoming SEC regulations enacted on December 18th.`** — regulation-timed hook. The cameo regulation here IS the spine because the spine pain (#3 disclosure clock) IS the regulation. Note: SEC rule enacted "on December 18th" with date specificity.
- **`It contains a guide for how to ensure you maintain compliance and a checklist for what to do if your organization encounters a potential incident, and the actions you can take.`** — describes the value vehicle (a checklist PDF) concretely. Action-oriented language: "ensure you maintain compliance," "what to do," "actions you can take."
- **`Originally the team and I created this resource for our public enterprise customers to be a companion piece for our platform that continuously monitors, measures, and communicates the value of their security program...`** — implied social proof ("our public enterprise customers") + concrete capability.
- **`I wanted to share it with you with zero upfront ask to see if it can provide value.`** — explicit zero-ask framing. Disarms the "this is a sales pitch" reflex.
- **`Don't like clicking links? I'd be happy to show it to you when timely and relevant.`** — soft secondary ask. Lower-friction than a calendar request.
- Sign-off with first name.

**15-year-old gets:**
- The SEC made new rules on December 18th about telling the public when you get hacked.
- Yosef made a checklist explaining the rules and what to do if it happens.
- He's sharing it for free, no strings attached.
- If Harshal doesn't want to click a link, Yosef will walk him through it.

**Pattern abstract (regulation-timed value-first play):**
1. Greeting (first-name comma preferred).
2. Optional one-line nicety (drop unless seasonal).
3. Regulation hook with EXACT enactment date.
4. One-sentence concrete description of the value resource.
5. Implied social proof + concrete capability ("originally created for our [profile-match] customers...").
6. Explicit zero-ask framing.
7. Optional secondary lower-friction ask.
8. Sign-off.

**When to use this pattern instead of the others:**
- Regulation just dropped (within last 30 days).
- You have an actual resource (PDF, checklist, framework) to send.
- The recipient is at a regulated public company.

## What every canonical example shares

Reading the seven side-by-side, the spine of Miles's voice is:

1. **First name + comma open.** Always. Never "Hi" except in the Yosef CWT example (which is older voice).

2. **Concrete trigger sentence, sentence 2.** A fact specific to THEIR company with a date or dollar figure or named person. NOT generic ("noticed your transformation"). NOT regulatory only ("the new HIPAA rule"). Actual news.

3. **Pattern Statement, sentence 3.** Miles's signature framing. Names the pain in language the recipient already uses to themselves. "Quiet killer." "Different animal." "Concrete number for the board." "Three audits, three formats, one team rebuilding the spreadsheet." Plain English. Memorable.

4. **Customer quote (verbatim, block-formatted)** when the recipient profile matches. The 160-hours line for healthcare. The 40-hours line for fintech. Hashal's "intuitive" line for tool-sprawl objections. Profile-match in the setup sentence so the recipient sees themselves.

5. **Sivan-as-former-CISO, ONE sentence.** Not a paragraph. "Sivan Tehila, our CEO, was a CISO before building this." That's the line. Don't pitch Sivan further.

6. **Onyxia-response in plain English.** Verb + concrete object + "so you don't [concrete time/effort waste]." Never "continuous control monitoring." Never "governance trail." Concrete actions, concrete metrics.

7. **Peer-framed ask.** "Compare notes." "On your radar." "Want me to set it up?" Single binary question. Specific timeframe when possible.

8. **Sign-off block** (locked 2026-05-04): four-line `Cheers, / Miles Bastianich / Onyxia Cyber / 203-524-8880`.

## What every canonical example AVOIDS

Reading the seven side-by-side, the things never present:

- **Regulation salad.** Never more than ONE regulation cited per email. Often zero. Triggers are events (settlement, hire, M&A) or signals (board change), not regulations.
- **Abstract nouns.** "Posture," "visibility," "coverage," "trail," "framework evidence" — none of these appear as the verb's object.
- **Two ideas per sentence.** Each sentence has one idea. Sentence boundaries are clean.
- **Self-referential wind-up.** "I wanted to reach out," "I noticed," "I came across" — none.
- **Closing wind-up.** "Looking forward to hearing from you," "Talk soon" — never.
- **Calendar links** in the first cold email. Single binary ask, no link dump.
- **Three-option time menus.** "Tuesday or Thursday" is two; never three.
- **Feature dumps.** One Onyxia capability per email. Whichever fits THIS pain for THIS person.
